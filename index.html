2026 · 06 · 23 LOG 005

A pack you have to actually pack

Built the inventory, and refused to make it a list of slots. It's a grid — every item has a real footprint, a trail camera eats a two-by-two block, and you rotate things with a right-click to make them fit. Drag a piece out of your pack and into a box on the ground; the two grids sit side by side and you ferry gear between them like you're packing for a trip you might not come back from. Which, in this valley, you might not.

Underneath it, every object in the world is real physics until the moment you pick it up — then it goes quiet and folds into the grid with all its settings intact, and comes back out into your hands the same object it was. You can also just grab things and carry them around, bump them off walls, throw them. A backpack is itself only another object: drop it, hand it to someone, loot it.

Most of the night was the unglamorous part — making the mouse let go of the camera at the right moment, making one key mean pick-up and another mean drop, making the cursor lock and unlock when it's supposed to instead of fighting four systems that all thought they owned it. Tore that last one out and gave the cursor a single boss. Two and a half hours; the most machinery I've ever stood up in one sitting. The grid looks rough. Tomorrow it gets dressed.

2026 · 06 · 21 LOG 004

Made the sun obey the laws of physics

Spent the back half of a Saturday night convincing a directional light to behave like an actual star. The sun in the valley now computes its real elevation and azimuth from latitude, day-of-year, and time of day — so at 38.2°N it peaks in the southern sky, not straight overhead, and the days get genuinely longer in summer and shorter in winter because the geometry says so.

Verified it against the real numbers: January 1 rises around 120° (southeast), barely clears 28° at noon, sets around 240°. June rises north of east, rides high, lingers late. It took a few wrong sign flips at 1 in the morning to get east and west sorted out — but it's right now, and seasons drop straight in by changing one number.

Most games in this corner fake the sun on a fixed arc. This one obeys celestial mechanics. Nobody will consciously notice. Everybody will feel it.

2026 · 06 · 20 LOG 003

Trail cameras you can actually service

The first real piece of the core loop. You can walk up to a trail camera in the field, lean in, and bring up a low-framerate aiming feed on its little screen — then read every camera's feed back at the base terminal, switching between them by name from a dropdown. Only the camera you're looking at renders, so it stays cheap.

The terminal grew a proper tabbed interface along the way: any new screen is just a panel named the right way and it gets discovered automatically. Adding the eventual review system later won't cost a line of plumbing.

2026 · 06 · 19 LOG 002

A real valley, walked at one-to-one

Pulled real USGS elevation data of the Greenbrier valley near Kennison, West Virginia, and rebuilt it in-engine at true scale — five kilometers across, three hundred and forty-nine meters of real relief. Standing at the bottom of the gorge looking up at terrain that an actual river carved is a different feeling than anything you can sculpt by hand.

The best part is free: the valley is split between folded, broken country on one side and a gentle plateau on the other — a real geologic boundary — and the whole risk economy of the game maps straight onto ground I didn't have to invent.

2026 · 06 · 18 LOG 001

First light

Starting a log. The Holler is a co-op folk-horror game set in a real Appalachian valley — you and up to three others contract for a wildlife-monitoring institute, go out into country that doesn't want you there, place equipment, and bring back data that doesn't always tell the truth.

This is the most progress I've ever made on a game, and writing it down is partly to keep myself honest and partly because the late-night problems are worth remembering. More soon.
